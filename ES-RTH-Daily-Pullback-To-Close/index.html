The Pullback-to-Close Entry Study

Flipping the R:R by waiting for price to return to yesterday's close before entering gap continuation trades.

What this study does: Avenue 3 established that under ABR-from-close, gap direction is the only factor that matters — prior bar direction, IBS, bar type, and bar size all collapsed to irrelevant. This study asks the next question: on a gap day, what if price pulls back to yesterday's close before continuing? Does that change the probability of hitting the measured move target, and does it change the risk? We ignore whether yesterday was a bull or bear bar. The only inputs are: gap direction, gap size, and whether the low (gap up) or high (gap down) touches yesterday's close during today's session.
Section 01The Pullback Paradox
Question: On gap days, how often does price come back to yesterday's close? And when it does, does that help or hurt the chance of reaching the 1× ABR target?

We split every gap day into two groups: days where price returned to the prior close during the session ("pullback"), and days where it never looked back ("no pullback"). This reveals something counterintuitive.

Gap size matters: Small gaps (<20% ABR) pull back to the close 84–87% of the time — they barely gapped in the first place. Large gaps (>30% ABR) only pull back 39–41%, meaning 60% of the time a large gap just keeps going. When a large gap DOES pull back, 0.5× hit rates stay high (77–85%), so there's still residual momentum — just not enough for 1×.
  • If you see a large gap (>30% ABR) that never returns to the close: that's a trend day. Don't fight it. These hit 1× ABR 71–80% of the time. If you didn't enter near the open, the train has left.
  • If a large gap pulls back to the close: there's still momentum (0.5× hits 77–85%), but the 1× dream is mostly dead (10–22%). Trade the 0.5× bounce, not the full measured move.
  • Small gaps (<20% ABR) that pull back: they barely moved. The 0.5× hit rate is only 43–44%. Not worth trading as a gap setup.
Section 02True Risk — MAE Past the Close
Question: If you place a limit order at yesterday's close and get filled on the pullback, how far does the market move against you before it either works or fails?

MAE (Maximum Adverse Excursion) measures the worst the trade gets before it either works or fails. On a gap-up day where the low touches yesterday's close, the MAE is how far below the close the low actually went. This is the real risk of the pullback entry — not the stop you set, but what the market actually does to you.

The R:R flip is real. Phase I's problem was 1:0.5 R:R (risking a full prior range to make half a range). By waiting for the pullback to the close, median risk drops to 0.29–0.38× ABR while the reward to the 1× target stays at 1× ABR. That's a 2.6:1 to 3.5:1 R:R. The entry location — not the target — was always the missing piece.
Bear asymmetry again: Bear pullback days overshoot the close by less (0.29× median vs 0.38×). Sellers are tighter. This is consistent with every previous study — bearish setups have consistently better risk characteristics on ES.
  • Entry: Limit order at yesterday's close. On gap-up days, this means a buy limit below the open. On gap-down days, a sell limit above the open.
  • Stop zone: P75 MAE is 0.51–0.69× ABR past the close. A stop at 0.75× ABR past the close would survive 75–80% of pullbacks. With current ABR around 55–60 points, that's roughly 40–45 points of risk.
  • But remember: The R:R looks great, but the 1× win rate on pullback days is only 14–22%. You need the right target to make the maths work. See Section 4.

Section 04The Trader's Trade — 0.5× ABR Target
Question: The 1× target has beautiful R:R but terrible win rate on pullback days. What if we cut the target in half? Does 0.5× ABR from the close produce a positive-expectancy trade?

Professional scalpers target 0.5× ABR on counter-trend and range-bound moves, reserving the full 1× ABR target for strong with-trend entries. This section tests whether applying the 0.5× target to the pullback entry creates a structurally sound trade. The entry remains the same: limit order at yesterday's close, filled on the pullback. Only the profit target changes.

Why 0.5× Works

The pullback resets the R:R. Your risk is only 0.29–0.38× ABR (the MAE past the close), and the 0.5× target is 0.5× ABR away. You're not asking the market to do something extraordinary — you're just capturing the remaining momentum from a gap that hasn't fully died yet.

Why 1× Doesn't Work

If the day was powerful enough to travel a full ABR from the close, it almost certainly wouldn't have pulled back to the close first. Non-pullback days hit 1× at 64–75%. Pullback days hit 1× at only 10–22%. The pullback IS the information telling you 1× isn't coming.

Combined annual edge: Gap Down 0.5× contributes +0.38R × 22.1/yr = +8.4R. Gap Up 0.5× contributes +0.30R × 27.0/yr = +8.1R. Total: approximately +16.5R/year from 49 trades, with 77–85% win rate and 1.3–1.75:1 R:R. This is before execution refinement (intraday entry timing, tighter stops).
  • The trade: Gap >30% ABR. Place a limit order at yesterday's RTH close. When filled on the pullback, target 0.5× ABR in the gap's direction. Stop at 0.75× ABR past the close (covers P75 MAE).
  • In points (current vol): ABR ~55–60pts. Gap must be >17pts. Target ~28–30pts from close. Stop ~42–45pts from close.
  • Frequency: ~49 times per year (about once per week). Bear setups are higher quality (84.9% vs 77.3%), bull setups are more frequent (27/yr vs 22/yr).
  • Not all fills become trades: You need the gap >30% ABR AND the pullback to the close. Only 39–41% of large gap days pull back. Some days your limit just doesn't get hit — and that's fine, because those are the trend days that hit 1× without you.
Section 05Counter-Trend Gap Behavior
Question: In a bull trend (above 20 EMA), when the market gaps down, does the gap continue or does the trend reassert itself? And vice versa — in a bear trend, do gap-up days fade?

This is the "gap vs trend" question. When a gap goes against the short-term trend (20 EMA), which force wins? We break it down by gap size because the answer changes dramatically at the 30% ABR threshold.

The 30% threshold is critical. Below 30% ABR, the trend wins: counter-trend gaps fade to the close 70–76% of the time and only continue to 1× in 22–26% of cases. Above 30% ABR, the gap wins: continuation to 1× hits 53–58%. A large gap against the trend is NOT a buying (or selling) opportunity — it's a signal that something has changed.
The stall zone is real. Regardless of gap size, 37–45% of counter-trend gap days hit 0.5× in the gap direction but stall before reaching 1×. This "half-move" zone is where the trend reasserts itself. It's consistent in both directions and across gap sizes. This confirms the visual observation that counter-trend gaps travel about half an ABR before running out of energy.
  • Small counter-trend gap (20–30% ABR): Trend wins ~75% of the time. Price fades back to the close. This is a gap-fade trade: enter with the trend, target the close. Risk is small because the gap was small.
  • Large counter-trend gap (>30% ABR): Gap wins ~53–58% of the time. Do NOT automatically fade. The gap is telling you something the trend hasn't priced in yet. Wait and observe.
  • The stall zone (0.5× hit, 1× not): If you're in a with-trend position and the market gaps against you overnight, the likely worst case is 0.5× ABR against you. The gap will travel about half a day's range against the trend, then stall. That's where you reassess — not at the open.
Section 06Key Insights & Practical Takeaways

1. The Pullback IS the Information

A pullback to the close on a gap day is not a "second chance to enter." It's a signal that the gap's momentum is fading. Days that never pull back hit 1× at 64–75%. Days that do pull back hit 1× at only 10–22%. Treat the pullback as a different trade entirely, not a discounted version of the continuation trade.

2. The 0.5× Target Solves the R:R

Phase I found a 1:0.5 R:R problem. The pullback entry with a 0.5× ABR target flips this to 1.3–1.75:1 R:R at 77–85% win rate. The secret was always about entry location, not target distance. Enter during the pullback (compression), target a modest expansion (0.5×), and the maths work.

3. Trend Context Has One Big Use Case

Bear trend + gap down + pullback is the only setup where 1× has positive expectancy (33.1% at 3.1:1 = +0.12R). For the 0.5× trade, trend context adds little — the trade works in both trend states because you're just capturing residual gap momentum, not betting on a full measured move.

4. The 30% ABR Threshold Separates Two Worlds

Below 30% ABR: small gaps that almost always pull back and have mediocre continuation. Above 30% ABR: real gaps where the pullback/no-pullback split becomes meaningful. Counter-trend gaps below 30% fade; above 30% they continue. This threshold appears consistently across every analysis.

5. Non-Pullback Days Are Trend Days

When a large gap (>30% ABR) doesn't pull back to the close, that's a trend day hitting 1× at 71–80%. You cannot trade these from the close. They require an entry near the open or not at all. Accepting that you will miss these days is part of the strategy — you're trading the OTHER 39–41% of days where the gap pauses.

6. Bear Setups Are Structurally Superior

Bear gap pullbacks have tighter MAE (0.29× vs 0.38×), higher 0.5× hit rates (84.9% vs 77.3%), and better R:R (1.75:1 vs 1.31:1). This bear advantage has appeared in every study we've run. Fear moves markets more efficiently than greed on ES.
